How to Read This Analysis

Correlation Matrix

Each cell shows how closely two stocks move together based on their daily returns over the past 2 years. Possible values range from -1.0 to 1.0:

  • 1.0 - two stocks move completely together
  • 0.0 - there is zero relationship between movements
  • -1.0 - two stocks move completely opposite of each other

For a diversified portfolio, you want stocks with a correlation closer to 0. FAANG stocks tend to be highly correlated since they are part of the same tech sector.

To calculate the correlation coefficient between two stocks, you find the covariance of the daily stock returns and divide that number by the product of those returns' standard deviations.

Note: Covariance measures how two things tend to move together, but by itself it isn't standardized from -1 to 1, which is why we use the correlation coefficient.

Sharpe Ratio

The Sharpe ratio measures how much return a stock delivered per unit of risk. It's calculated as:

Sharpe = (Annual Return - Risk-free Rate) / Annual Volatility

Here, our annual return is calculated as the mean daily return over every trading day the past two years multiplied by 252 (average number of trading days per year).
The risk-free rate we use is the 3 month Treasury bill rate (currently about 3.7%), and the annual volatility is the mean standard deviation in returns each day multiplied by the square root of 252.

You may notice that the Sharpe ratios for several of these FAANG stocks are actually fairly low. This is in part due to their high volatilities.
